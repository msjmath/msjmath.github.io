By Aaron | Published April 25, 2013

We talked about a ubiquitous 'Fact 5' today. (See the handout.) Many of the practice problems come from recent competitions, which goes to show how powerful this lemma is. If you are approaching Olympiad math, this is one of the must-knows for geometry section.

Here are hints:

1) Symmetry.
2) After making some observations from Fact 5, use the result from #3 and observe the range of angle BCD.
3) Angle chase.
4) Drop another perpendicular.
5) 5 and 10 degree angles are not very nice. Keep chasing angles until you find something nicer to use with lengths.
6) You won't need Fact 5, but the problem is good practice for this configuration. Use Power of a Point on the intersection of the chords.
7) Focus on how you would chase lengths in some incircle configurations. There are a few ways to finish up the problem after you have the perimeter of the triangle.
8) What theorem is useful for cyclic quadrilaterals side lengths?

Week 16: Olympiad Angle Chasing

By Aaron | Published April 18, 2013

This week we talked about angle chasing. For USA(J)MO participants, this is likely one of the most useful techniques you can use when approaching olympiad geometry. The key to many of the problems is to pay attention to when certain configurations of diagrams are angle-chase-able; this takes a bit of practice however. Cyclic quadrilaterals were also discussed, a very useful tool for angle chasing due to the multiple angle relationships.

Here are some hints:

2.1: Assume that two altitudes intersect at point H, and show that the third altitude passes through H. How does one use angles to show that three points are collinear?
2.2: Use the arcs of the circumcircle.
2.3: How do you show that a quadrilateral is cyclic?
2.4: Lots of right angles usually means that there are lurking cyclic quadrilaterals.
2.5: Split this problem up into proving 4 points are cyclic at a time. First the three midpoints and one foot should prove the six point circle, and then the feet and the midpoint of HA.

3.1: Right angles = cyclic quadrilaterals! Keep your eye on which angles you want to show are congruent.
3.2: You must prove that several quadrilaterals are cyclic.
3.3: When two circles are tangent, the two centers and the tangency/intersection point are collinear. Use lemma 2.2.
3.4: Be careful.
3.5: You will need a little bit more than angle chasing. Do any points in your pretty diagram look like they just might be on the same circle?
3.6: See the parts of the problem in MMT Power Round.

Week 10: Cubics and an Identity

By Aaron | Published February 11, 2013

This week, we discussed a very famous identity (that apparently does not have any name), and related it to cubic polynomials on computational math contests. As we discovered during the lecture, the cubic formula has its weaknesses - if you try applying it directly to the polynomial generated from the example problem, we end up with the original root. In other words, the cubic formula does not always simplify for you. We also discussed a very nice derivation of the cubic formula (not Cardano's Method!) using the identity. Details are posted in the handout. However, this method does not avoid simplification errors either.

As always, here are some hints and comments:

1) Let a = 2000 and b = 1999. Factor.
2) Move the 5 to the left hand side.
3) Break the cubic up into a few cases depending on the signs of the factors.
4) What is P_2(x) in terms of P_0(x)? Generalize to P_20(x).
5) Clear denominators. What do three of those coefficients remind you of?
6) Fit this expression to the identity.
7) Have not found solution yet. :P I will update once I find it. (Link for Solution)
8) It's a horrible problem. After some computation, you end up finding that you have to guess and check. It's still good practice though.

Week 8: Incircles

By Aaron | Published February 10, 2013

This week, we introduced the idea behind an incircle, and some basic tricks when it comes to approaching computational problems about incircles. The most prevalent trick when it comes to working with incircles comes as part of the proof of that Area = semiperimeter * inradius. This not only shows that the sides of a triangle can be written as a+b, b+c, and c+a for some positive a,b,c, but also demonstrates the technique of recognizing the symmetry of the incircle about angle bisectors.

Here are hints and other remarks to problems on this week's handout:

  1. Basic formula application. Random note: do all right triangles with integer side lengths have integer inradii?
  2. Split BIC into two triangles. (Yay symmetry!)
  3. Yay a NIMO problem. Look at the proof for in the second point. How do we know that two tangents to the incircle from the same point are congruent? Is there something similar that can be said for this problem? (Symmetry again!)
  4. If DE is a perpendicular bisector, what do you know about AD and AE? (Symmetry once again)
  5. How do you chase lengths cut off by incircle tangencies? Also, set a variable.
  6. Take advantage of equal tangent segments again. (Symmetry strikes once more!) It may help to express AK = AF - FK, where F is the tangency point of AD to one of the circles, since AK is not a very nice segment length.
  7. First, work with n-1, n, and n+1 instead. Try to split the sides into something you can work with (like with #5).
  8. Draw a good diagram. Does anything look... collinear?
