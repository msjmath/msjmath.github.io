Week 7: Bijections

By Jeffrey | Published February 10, 2013

We covered bijections this week. Bijections are a one-to-one correspondence between two sets. Often, in combinatorics problems, one set is the set of solutions that we are trying to find, the other (unknown) set is a set which is significantly easier to count, and the challenge is to find a bijection between the solution set and the unknown set and thereby find the unknown set and the answer. Now that may be overly formal, but here are some examples from the handout:

Paths: the unknown set is the number of ways of permuting a string of 4 Rs and 5 Us, and the bijection is R <-> +1 in x direction, U <-> +1 in y direction.
Balls and urns:  the unknown set is the number of ways of

Note that quite a few of these problems can be solved by bashing. The bijective solutions are worth seeking, even if they are often less straightforward to find, because they drastically reduce computation (and thus careless mistakes), are a way to check your work, and are more elegant.

Also note that not all of these problems can be solved by using bijections. Bijections are not a panacea for your combinatorial troubles, but one of many tools you can use to attack math problems. You should develop some intuition for when bijections are feasible to find.

Examples:

1. This problem illustrates one method of solving quadratics by "completing the square" to get a difference of squares.
2. One of my favorite problems. The lesson from this solution is that you should look for something that comes up on both sides - namely, the number 5. This could give you ideas as to how you would turn a quartic into a quadratic.

Week 4: Mods and Digits

By Aaron | Published October 13, 2012

This meeting, we took a look at a few problems concerning mods and digits type problems. This handout was a bit difficult for AMC level, but they are quite instructive as to how you would approach the problems. Here are some hints and comments.

1: There are only so many different remainders for 3^x in mod 8. Try the first few values and see when it repeats. Another interesting note: let's say that you condensed it using a geometric series. What does this tell you about how you can take big exponents and find the remainder?

2: Think about divisibility for 3 and 5 separately.

3: This problem is a little bit tricky. Since you want nonzero digits, it would help to separate the multiples of 5 from everything else. (Keep in mind that all of the 5's are used for making zeros at the end.) To find the last two digits, break it into mod 4 and mod 25. You may need to break into periodicities if that helps.

4: Mod 9, Mod 11, etc. will help. However, there's a fast way to do this by looking at 7, 11, 13... (discussed in class)

5: There are a few cases you'll have to go through, but how do we split a number up into "first digit" and "everything else"?

6: Periodicities. If you keep dividing by 2 when would you hit a 4? When do you not? And what does it mean to "get rid of a digit"?

7: First convince yourself that a set of numbers will repeat over at some point, after 1, 2 and 4. (Why do we have to take this into account?) Suppose that your set of distinct residuals before it repeats is a1, a2, ..., an. Now what happens when you multiply by 2?

8: Once you get the most efficient algorithm, find a nice way to condense the sum. When you had a long expression like 1+2+...+100 or 1+2+4+...+2^100, what did you do?

9: Sum of digits makes you think of what mod...? But that alone isn't enough. Could you bound the value of a and b?

Week 1: Areas

By Aaron | Published September 21, 2012

This week, we showed you some problems involving area that we think are pretty nice. We attached the handout on this post, if you lost your copy from math club or were unable to attend. We hope that you enjoyed these problems!

Here are a few comments regarding the problems (spoiler alert!)

 

Example 1: Read the title of the handout. To those pursuing USA(J)MO this year, in this kind of problem where it asks if something is possible and you think it isn't, it would help to look for something called an invariant, or something that doesn't change (area in this case). We will go into more detail about that later.
Example 2: There is an error in terms of the numbers (if you work it out, you will get cos > 1, since I didn't check the numbers when I made this problem up. This problem is a nice application of how to use area to set up equations nicely.

 

Problem 1: What happens to the overlap region?
Problem 2: How do you find the area between two circles?
Problem 3: This problem has a lot of different solutions, but we covered a pretty interesting technique in class. Consider a room ABCD with the two carpets AND and CMD. Notice that some areas of the room have 0, 1, and 2 pieces of carpets on them. How do we know that the region with 2 pieces of carpet is the same as the area with no carpet on it?
Problem 4: Find two points that work, and prove it for everything "in between". Be sure that you also talk about why other points don't work.
Problem 5: Look at the area of the region common to both rectangles.
Problem 6: This was a pretty interesting and instructive problem, but we didn't have time to cover it. Break the figure into two parts with line KM (work on each side separately). Think about how you should express the area of triangle KLM in terms of other triangles without the letter L in it.
Problem 7: This problem is significantly harder than the rest. As a hint, incenters and areas... hmm... what does that motivate?
